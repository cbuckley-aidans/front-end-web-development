Making Websites Work for Everyone

Imagine trying to read a website where the text is so faint you can barely see it, or trying to use a button that's impossible to click. For many people, these aren't just annoying problems - they're barriers that can make websites completely unusable.

Making Things Easy to See: Colour Contrast

Colour contrast is like making sure your writing stands out on paper. Just as you wouldn't write with yellow pencil on white paper, websites need to use colours that are easy to see together.

About 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women have some form of colour blindness. Good contrast helps everyone, but it's especially important for people with visual impairments.

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines contain some great information on how to check if your websites are accessible.

One of the things they provide is a formula to check if your colour contrast is accessible or not.

Colour Communication and Palettes

Good colour choices aren't just about contrast - they're about creating a balanced, professional look. The 60:30:10 rule is a designer's best friend:

60% Primary

Your main colour, used for backgrounds and large areas

30% Secondary

Supporting colour for sections and components

10% Accent

Highlight colour for buttons and important elements

CARP Design Principles

CARP stands for Contrast, Alignment, Repetition, and Proximity. These principles guide effective visual design.

CARP helps us make user interfaces that just make sense. By lowering cognitive load (or thinking time), your users can use your interface quicker and easier.

Understanding CARP Principles

Contrast: Creates visual hierarchy and draws attention to important elements.

Alignment: Organizes elements along invisible lines for a cleaner layout.

Repetition: Uses consistent styles and elements to unify the design.

Proximity: Groups related items together to show relationships.

Making Things Clear: ARIA Labels and Alt Text

ARIA labels and alt text are like invisible signs that screen readers use to explain what things are on a webpage.

Use WIN + CTRL + ENTER on your keyboard to try this out now. Navigate through the examples using the TAB key to see how they compare.

Alt Text Example
<!-- Bad example: no alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>

<!-- Good example: descriptive alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
alt="A cute kitten with a pink background"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>
Example Image

Screen Reader Test

Without alt text, screen readers might say "image" or skip it entirely. With alt text, users understand what the image shows.

ARIA Label Example
<!-- Bad example: unclear button -->
<button>×</button>

<!-- Good example: clear purpose -->
<button 
aria-label="Close menu"
class="btn btn-circle">
<span class="sr-only">
Close Menu
</span>
×
</button>
