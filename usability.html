Making Websites Work for Everyone

Learn about accessibility, design principles, and usability

Introduction

Imagine trying to read a website where the text is so faint you can barely see it, or trying to use a button that's impossible to click.

For many people, these aren't just annoying problems - they're barriers that can make websites completely unusable.

This page demonstrates best practices to use when designing your own website, as well as ways to check if your website is accessible to diverse users.

About This Design: Glassmorphism

This page uses a design trend called glassmorphism, characterized by:

  • Frosted glass effect with translucency
  • Subtle borders that separate elements
  • Soft shadows and subtle lighting
  • Vibrant background colors that shine through

Glassmorphism creates a sense of depth and dimension while allowing content to stand out clearly. On this page, it helps us:

  • Organize content into clear, separate sections
  • Highlight interactive elements with visual depth
  • Create a modern, visually engaging experience

Colour & Contrast

Why Contrast Matters

About 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women have some form of colour blindness. Good contrast helps everyone, but it's especially important for people with visual impairments.

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide a formula to check if your colour contrast is accessible or not.

Colour Palettes: The 60:30:10 Rule

Good colour choices aren't just about contrast - they're about creating a balanced, professional look. The 60:30:10 rule is commonly used by designers:

60% Primary

Your main colour, used for backgrounds and large areas

30% Secondary

Supporting colour for sections and components

10% Accent

Highlight colour for buttons and important elements

ARIA Labels and Alt Text

ARIA labels and alt text are like invisible signs that screen readers use to explain what things are on a webpage.

Alt Text Example
<!-- Bad example: no alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>

<!-- Good example: descriptive alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
alt="A cute kitten with a pink background"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>
Example Image

Screen Reader Test

Without alt text, screen readers might say "image" or skip it entirely. With alt text, users understand what the image shows.

ARIA Label Example
<!-- Bad example: unclear button -->
<button>×</button>

<!-- Good example: clear purpose -->
<button 
aria-label="Close menu"
class="btn btn-circle">

Close Menu

Typography & Spacing

Good typography and spacing improves readability and helps users scan content efficiently.

Poor Readability

This paragraph has tight line spacing and extends across the full width of its container. When lines of text are too long and too close together, it becomes difficult for readers to track from the end of one line to the beginning of the next. This creates cognitive strain and reduces reading comprehension.

Good Readability

This paragraph has comfortable line spacing and a controlled line length. The ideal line length is around 65 characters per line. Combined with proper line height, this makes text easy to read and navigate.

CARP Design Principles

Understanding CARP

CARP stands for Contrast, Alignment, Repetition, and Proximity. These principles guide effective visual design.

Contrast

Text and content should be easily readable. Important things should stand out.

Alignment

Elements should be aligned with each other to create order and organization. Nothing should be placed arbitrarily on the page.

Repetition

Reuse visual elements throughout the design to create unity and consistency. Similar functions should look similar.

Proximity

Group related items together visually. Physical closeness implies a relationship between elements, making information easier to understand and scan.

CARP helps us make user interfaces that just make sense. By lowering cognitive load (or thinking time), your users can use your interface quicker and easier.

Usability Principles

5 Core Usability Principles

Another way of ensuring our web experiences are accessible is by evaluating them using these 5 useability principles:

Accessibility

Ability to be used by many different people, even people with disabilities

Effectiveness

Ability of users to use the system to do the work they need to do, includes reliability

Safety

Ability for users to make errors and recover from the mistake

Utility

Ability of the system to provide all the functionality that users need

Learnability

How easy a system is to learn
