Making Websites Work for Everyone

Imagine trying to read a website where the text is so faint you can barely see it, or trying to use a button that's impossible to click.

For many people, these aren't just annoying problems - they're barriers that can make websites completely unusable.

This page has some examples of best practices to use when designing your own website, as well as ways to check if your website is accessible to diverse users.

Making Things Easy to See: Colour Contrast

About 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women have some form of colour blindness. Good contrast helps everyone, but it's especially important for people with visual impairments.

One of the things the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines provide is a formula to check if your colour contrast is accessible or not.

Colour Communication and Palettes

Good colour choices aren't just about contrast - they're about creating a balanced, professional look. The 60:30:10 rule is commonly used by designers to purposefully reduce the number of colours used:

60% Primary

Your main colour, used for backgrounds and large areas

30% Secondary

Supporting colour for sections and components

10% Accent

Highlight colour for buttons and important elements

Line Height & Width

Poor Readability

This paragraph has tight line spacing and extends across the full width of its container. When lines of text are too long and too close together, it becomes difficult for readers to track from the end of one line to the beginning of the next. This creates cognitive strain and reduces reading comprehension.

Good Readability

This paragraph has comfortable line spacing and a controlled line length. The ideal line length is around 65 characters per line. Combined with proper line height, this makes text easy to read and navigate.

Spacing Tips

  • Use padding to create space between an element's border and its content
  • Maintain consistent spacing throughout your design

Making Things Clear: ARIA Labels and Alt Text

ARIA labels and alt text are like invisible signs that screen readers use to explain what things are on a webpage.

Use WIN + CTRL + ENTER on your keyboard to try this out now. Navigate through the examples using the TAB key to see how they compare.

Alt Text Example
<!-- Bad example: no alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>

<!-- Good example: descriptive alt text -->
<img 
src="images/cat.webp"
alt="A cute kitten with a pink background"
class="rounded shadow-lg"
/>
Example Image

Screen Reader Test

Without alt text, screen readers might say "image" or skip it entirely. With alt text, users understand what the image shows.

ARIA Label Example
<!-- Bad example: unclear button -->
<button>×</button>

CARP Design Principles

CARP stands for Contrast, Alignment, Repetition, and Proximity. These principles guide effective visual design.

Contrast: Things that LOOK different should BE different. This draws attention to important elements.

Alignment: Things that belong together should line up.

Repetition: The opposite of contrast. All blue buttons should do the same thing, for example.

Proximity: Related items should be together to show relationships.

CARP helps us make user interfaces that just make sense. By lowering cognitive load (or thinking time), your users can use your interface quicker and easier.

Usability Principles in Action

Another way of ensuring our web experiences are accessible is by evaluating them using the 5 useability principles:

  • Accessibility: Ability to be used by many different people, even people with disabilities
  • Effectiveness: Ability of users to use the system to do the work they need to do, includes reliability
  • Safety: Ability for users to make errors and recover from the mistake
  • Utility: Ability of the system to provide all the functionality that users need
  • Learnability: How easy a system is to learn
